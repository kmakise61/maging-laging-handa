üè• Basic First Aid Guide

Essential first aid procedures for common disaster-related injuries

Individual guides can be printed from within each section

‚ö†Ô∏è EMERGENCY: For severe injuries, bleeding that won't stop, difficulty breathing, or loss of consciousness - CALL 911 IMMEDIATELY

ü©π Cuts & Wounds

SEVERITY: Minor to Moderate | COLOR CODE: Blue/Green

üîç Assessment:

  • Check for embedded foreign objects (do not remove deep objects)
  • Assess severity: Minor (shallow, small) vs. Severe (deep, large, won't stop bleeding)
  • Look for signs of infection: redness, warmth, swelling, pus

üìã Step-by-Step Care:

  1. Stay calm and ask person to sit or lie down
  2. Check for embedded objects - do not remove if deeply embedded
  3. Apply direct pressure with clean cloth for 2-3 minutes minimum
  4. Maintain pressure until bleeding stops completely
  5. Cover with clean bandage once bleeding has stopped
  6. Monitor for infection over next few days

üö® Seek Emergency Care If:

  • Bleeding won't stop after 10 minutes of direct pressure
  • Deep wound exposing bone, muscle, or fat
  • Wound on face, hands, or joints
  • Signs of infection develop (redness, warmth, pus, red streaks)
  • Wound caused by dirty or rusty object (tetanus risk)
Source: Philippine Red Cross Community-Based Health First Aid

üî• Burns

SEVERITY: Minor to Critical | COLOR CODE: Red/Orange

üéØ Burn Classification:

1st Degree (Minor)

Red, painful, no blisters. Affects only outer layer of skin.

2nd Degree (Moderate)

Red, blistered, very painful. Affects outer and underlying layer.

3rd Degree (Severe)

White/charred, may not be painful. Affects all layers. CALL 911!

‚úÖ For MINOR Burns (1st/2nd degree, small area):

  1. Cool with clean running water for 15-20 minutes until pain reduces
  2. Remove jewelry/clothing if NOT stuck to skin
  3. Do NOT open blisters - they protect from infection
  4. Do NOT apply cream, ointment, or ice
  5. Cover with clean, dry bandage (non-adhesive preferred)
  6. Take pain reliever if needed (paracetamol or ibuprofen)

üö® For SEVERE Burns (3rd degree, large area):

  1. CALL 911 IMMEDIATELY
  2. Do NOT remove burnt clothing
  3. Do NOT immerse in water (shock risk for large burns)
  4. Cover with moist, clean cloth
  5. Keep person warm with blanket (don't overheat)
  6. Monitor breathing and be prepared for CPR

üö® Go to Hospital Immediately If:

  • Burn on face, hands, feet, genitals, or major joints
  • Burn larger than your palm
  • 3rd degree burn (white/charred skin)
  • Burn caused by chemicals or electricity
  • Person is infant, elderly, or has chronic illness
Source: Philippine Red Cross, American Red Cross

ü¶µ Sprains & Strains

SEVERITY: Minor to Moderate | COLOR CODE: Yellow/Orange

‚ùÑÔ∏è RICE Method:

R
Rest

Avoid using the injured area. Use crutches if needed for leg/ankle injuries.

I
Ice

Apply ice pack for 15-20 minutes every 2-3 hours for first 48 hours. Use cloth between ice and skin.

C
Compression

Wrap with elastic bandage (not too tight - should not cut off circulation).

E
Elevation

Raise injured area above heart level to reduce swelling.

üö® Seek Medical Care If:

  • Severe pain or can't bear weight on injured limb
  • Numbness or tingling in the area
  • Severe swelling or bruising
  • No improvement after 48 hours of RICE treatment
  • Hearing/feeling a "pop" at time of injury (possible fracture)
  • Joint looks deformed or out of place

ü¶¥ Fractures (Broken Bones)

SEVERITY: Moderate to Severe | COLOR CODE: Purple/Red

‚ö†Ô∏è Signs of Possible Fracture:

  • Severe pain that worsens with movement
  • Swelling, bruising, or deformity
  • Unable to move the affected area
  • Bone visible through skin (open fracture - CALL 911)
  • Grinding sensation or sound

üè• Immediate Care:

  1. Call 911 or seek emergency care immediately
  2. Do NOT try to realign the bone
  3. Immobilize the area - keep it still, don't move
  4. Apply ice pack (with cloth barrier) to reduce swelling
  5. Treat for shock - keep person warm and calm
  6. If open fracture - cover wound with sterile bandage, don't clean it

ü©∫ Simple Splinting (if medical help is delayed):

  • Use rigid material (board, stick, rolled newspaper)
  • Pad the splint with cloth
  • Immobilize joints above and below fracture
  • Secure with bandages or cloth strips
  • Check circulation - fingers/toes should be warm and pink

‚ö†Ô∏è DO NOT:

  • Move the person unless absolutely necessary
  • Try to straighten or realign the bone
  • Test range of motion
  • Give food or drink (may need surgery)

‚ö° Shock (Medical Emergency)

SEVERITY: CRITICAL - Life Threatening | COLOR CODE: Dark Red/Critical

üëÅÔ∏è Recognition Signs:

  • Pale, cold, clammy skin
  • Rapid, weak pulse
  • Rapid, shallow breathing
  • Weakness, dizziness, or confusion
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Loss of consciousness

üö® Immediate Management:

  1. CALL 911 IMMEDIATELY - Shock is life-threatening
  2. Lay person down on back
  3. Elevate legs about 12 inches (unless head, neck, back, or leg injury)
  4. Keep person warm with blanket or coat
  5. Do NOT give food or water
  6. Monitor breathing and pulse continuously
  7. Turn head to side if vomiting
  8. Provide reassurance - stay calm

üö® CRITICAL: CALL 911

Shock is a life-threatening medical emergency that requires immediate professional care. Do not delay calling emergency services.

üè• Leptospirosis & Dengue Information

What is Leptospirosis?

Leptospirosis is a bacterial infection transmitted through contaminated water or soil from rodent urine. It's especially common during floods and in areas with poor sanitation.

üìä 2025 Leptospirosis Statistics

Total Cases: 4,859 cases (June-September 2025)
üìà Increase: 43.4% vs same period 2024
üìä Breakdown: 2,396 cases (June 8 - August 7, 2025)
üì∞ Sources: DOH, ABS-CBN, Inquirer.net
üóìÔ∏è As of: September 5, 2025
üîÑ Update Frequency: Weekly via DOH
‚ö†Ô∏è High-Risk Period: Monsoon season (June-November)
DOH Official Website ‚Üí ABS-CBN Health News ‚Üí

Symptoms (appear 5-30 days after exposure)

üå°Ô∏è High Fever
üò´ Severe Headache
üí™ Muscle Aches
üëÅÔ∏è Red Eyes
ü§¢ Nausea
ü§Æ Vomiting

Prevention Methods

  • Avoid wading in floodwaters
  • Wear protective boots and clothing when necessary
  • Control rodent populations
  • Maintain proper sanitation
  • Cover wounds with waterproof bandages
  • Take prophylactic antibiotics if exposed (consult doctor)

‚ö†Ô∏è When to Seek Medical Care

Seek immediate medical attention if you develop fever, headache, and muscle pain after exposure to floodwater or areas with poor sanitation, especially within 5-30 days of exposure.

What is Dengue?

Dengue is a viral infection transmitted by Aedes aegypti mosquitoes. There are four serotypes (DENV-1, DENV-2, DENV-3, DENV-4), and infection with one does not provide immunity to others.

ü¶† Serotypes: DENV-1, DENV-2, DENV-3, DENV-4 all circulating

Symptoms (appear 3-14 days after mosquito bite)

üå°Ô∏è High Fever
üò´ Severe Headache
üëÅÔ∏è Pain Behind Eyes
üí™ Joint/Muscle Pain
üî¥ Skin Rash
ü§Æ Nausea/Vomiting

Prevention Methods

  • Eliminate mosquito breeding sites (stagnant water)
  • Use insect repellent regularly
  • Wear long-sleeved clothing
  • Install window and door screens
  • Practice the 4S strategy: Search and destroy, Self-protection, Seek early consultation, Support fogging

üö® Severe Dengue Warning Signs

Seek immediate emergency care if you experience:

  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Persistent vomiting
  • Bleeding from nose or gums
  • Vomiting blood
  • Rapid breathing or difficulty breathing
  • Fatigue or restlessness
